## Design Context

### Users

**Primary audience:** policy researchers, academics, policymakers, and civil society actors working on AI governance.

**Context of use:** desk research during working hours — cross-referencing country postures, pulling citations for briefs and papers, benchmarking one jurisdiction against peers. Often has ten tabs open and is in the middle of writing something. Reads carefully, distrusts marketing gloss, wants to verify claims against sources.

**Job to be done:** quickly form an accurate, comparable mental model of how different countries are regulating AI across six dimensions — and get from that model to primary sources without friction.

### Brand Personality

**Three words:** rigorous, calm, global.

**Tone:** measured authority. The site has a point of view (regulation varies meaningfully and deserves careful comparison) but does not shout it. Closer to a reference work than to a product. Treats the reader as a peer.

**Emotional goal:** trust. The researcher should feel they can cite this in a footnote without apologizing.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Reference neighborhood:** contemporary data-viz — Observable notebooks, MIT Media Lab, The Pudding's more restrained pieces, Our World in Data's clarity with more contemporary typography. Technical confidence without being cold.

**Theme:** ship both light and dark with a persistent user toggle. Light theme is the default for citation-friendly daylight reading; dark theme is for long sessions where the map colors can breathe against a low-luminance ground.

**Key moves:**
- The map is the protagonist. Every piece of UI chrome must justify its visual weight against it.
- Typography should pair a distinctive display/serif or grot with a precise neutral sans. Not Inter, not IBM Plex — look further. A quiet slab or a crisp neo-grotesque can both work.
- Neutrals tinted toward a single considered hue (likely a warm-leaning off-white in light; a desaturated deep blue-black in dark). No pure #000 or #fff.
- Color in the choropleth should be perceptually uniform (OKLCH) and colorblind-safe. Accent color used sparingly — selection, active filter, hover emphasis.
- Asymmetric, left-aligned layouts. The country panel should read like a structured reference entry, not a card.

**Anti-references — the site must NOT look like:**
- Generic SaaS / AI startup (purple gradients, glassmorphism, cyan-on-dark, feature-grid heroes)
- Corporate consultancy (stock imagery, thin display serifs, 2×2 matrices, "frameworks")
- Government portal cliché (flat-blue header, dated nav, accessibility bolted on)
- Crypto / web3 dashboard (neon accents, glow, chart-every-pixel)

### Design Principles

1. **The map is the protagonist.** Chrome, panels, and controls recede so the choropleth reads first. If a decoration competes with the map, it loses.

2. **Rigor over ornament.** Every visual element earns its place by clarifying information or guiding attention. No border-left stripes, no gradient text, no decorative sparklines. Numbers and sources do the heavy lifting.

3. **Designed for comparison.** Researchers rarely look at one country — they look at three, then five, then compare across a dimension. Layouts, controls, and the comparison view should make side-by-side reading the natural path, not a special mode.

4. **Citeable by default.** Every score, every claim, every description points to a primary source within one click. Confidence and last-updated metadata are visible, not buried. The site should feel like something you can drop a footnote on.

5. **Calm density.** Information-rich is the target; cluttered is the failure. Achieve density with typographic hierarchy, generous-but-varied whitespace, and restraint on color — not by removing substance.
